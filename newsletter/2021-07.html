Tor Connection assistant

From the feedback that we receive from users around the world, we knew, that connecting to Tor from Tails was problematic for our most vulnerable users: whistleblowers surveilled at work, domestic violence survivors surveilled at home, and people who use Tails to circumvent nationwide censorship, especially in Asia.

That's why we added a Tor Connection assistant to make it easier for them to connect to the Tor network in Tails 4.20 (July 13).

Despite all the work done on this feature since February, we received several reports from people who are not able to connect to Tor anymore. Unfortunately, the 3 beta versions that we published since April were not enough to detect these problems before the release. We are making it our top priority to fix as many of these bugs as possible in time for 4.21 (August 10).

This first release of the Tor Connection assistant is only a first step. We will add more improvements to it in Tails 4.22 (September 7):

  • Save Tor bridges to the Persistent Storage (#5461)

  • Help detect when Wi-Fi is not working (#14534)

  • Synchronize the clock to make it easier to use Tor bridges in Asia (#15548)

Persistent Storage redesign

The next big change in Tails will be a redesign of the Persistent Storage. This ongoing work is entirely funded by the donations that we received last year from all of you.

The Persistent Storage hasn't changed much since we released it first in 2012 because the current code is hard to modify and improve. But, we learned from a survey with users, that it could do a lot more for you if it had more features. It could also be much easier to use for newcomers.

We started working on a complete redesign and rewrite of the Persistent Storage that will make it easier to persist more settings in the future.

You can see how the new Persistent Storage will look like from the mockups in our blueprint.

For this work, like for all major features in Tails, we practiced user-centered design and tested our prototype with users to make sure that our prototype was as easy to use as possible. You can read more about our design methodology in our interview with Simply Secure about formative testing.

Get more out of your Tails

  • When doing automatic upgrades, the size of the download increases over time.

    You can reduce the size of the download of future automatic upgrades by doing a manual upgrade to the latest version.

  • Our new warnings page is shorter, easier to understand by less technical people, less scary, more helpful, and visually attractive. We also integrated it into our download page so that you can learn more about Tails while waiting for your download.

  • With the major update of OnionShare included in Tails 4.20, you can now host your own onion website directly from Tails.

  • You can start Tails from an external hard disk if you need more space or more speed for your Persistent Storage. To do so, remove the live-media=removable boot options when starting Tails.

They are using Tails

Sean Vegezzi, an artist from New York, uses Tails to investigates abuses of state power. He told our team:

When I'm thinking about researching anything to do with the abuse of state power, the first thing that comes to mind is that we are living in an epoch where even visiting a URL could be used against you as circumstantial evidence in a court case stemming from a journalism / research / art project. Autonomy is a vague concept at best, but Tails offers me the closest thing to autonomy when doing sensitive research.

Sean used Tails to investigate, together with Laura Poitras, obscured nodes of New York City's violent carceral and policing infrastructure, like this 191-meter prison ship in the South Bronx where 600 people are being held in terrifying conditions.

Laura and Sean, together with Forensics Architecture, are exposing more of their investigations on the abuse of state power in their Berlin expo Circles.
